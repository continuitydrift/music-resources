Harmony

The Core Progression

A minor ii–V loop in D minor, extended to 8 bars. The tonic Dm7 arrives only once (bar 3) and never settles — the progression keeps cycling through tension rather than resolution. The final two bars handle the turnaround back to bar 1, with two alternating versions for variety.

Full Form

A · Breakdown · Bridge · A

Bars 1–6 oscillate between A7♭9 and B♭7♭9 — a half-step pendulum, never resolving. Bar 7 shifts to Db7 (frets 6-6-8-7), bar 8 to E7 (frets 9-9-11-10, same shape +3 frets). These two chords are a minor third apart on the diminished axis — symmetrically related, sharing harmonic tension without either resolving. Hold bar 8 and stop completely: full caesura before returning to Em7♭5 at the top of A.
Breakdown · 1–2 passes of the A section
Same 8-bar chord sequence as the A section, but stripped to upright bass + one mandolin only (whichever is playing chords). All other instruments drop out — no percussion, no melody, no hand-pan. The harmonic structure remains intact but the texture is skeletal. This serves two purposes: it creates contrast and rest before the bridge, and it makes the full re-entry at the end feel like an arrival. One pass of breakdown is usually enough; two if the mood calls for more space.

The breakdown (pass 5) keeps only octave mandolin and upright bass — the harmonic skeleton, no rhythm or color. Lowest energy point of the piece; makes the bridge feel like a genuine departure. The hand-pan is featured in the bridge rather than throughout the A section — its timbre suits the oscillating A7♭9/B♭7♭9 pendulum and the Db7→E7 caesura particularly well. The cello enters in the bridge and carries into the return, giving the ending more weight than the opening had.

The "swap" row reflects your practice of alternating which mandolin carries chords vs. melody each pass — keeps the texture alive without adding new instruments.

Instrument Notes

Roles & Starting Points

🎸 Octave Mandolin Harmony
  • Chords sit below the melody register — natural default for harmony
  • Em7♭5: frets 0,0,1,1 · A7♭9: frets 2,2,4,3 · Dm7: frets 2,2,3,3
  • Let chords ring — don't strum aggressively, let the sustain do the work
  • On bar 8 turnaround, consider just the chromatic approach note rather than all three
🎵 Regular Mandolin Melody
  • D natural minor (Aeolian): D E F G A B♭ C D
  • The ♭9 (E♭) over A7♭9 is a color note worth landing on deliberately
  • Bar 7 (two bars of A7♭9) is space to develop a melodic idea and resolve it
  • Swap chord/melody roles every pass to keep texture varied
🥁 Cajon + Shakers Pulse
  • Enter on pass 3 — let harmony establish first
  • Shakers slightly left or right of center; cajon centered
  • At 70–90 bpm, a laid-back feel suits the D minor mood better than driving
  • Consider leaving bar 8 (turnaround) slightly sparse — let the harmonic shift breathe
🎻 Upright Bass Foundation
  • Root notes on beat 1 is enough to start — E, A, D, A, E, A, A, B♭/A
  • Two-feel when ready: root on 1, fifth on 3 (E→B♭, A→E, D→A)
  • Bars 6–7 (both A7♭9): try walking A–B–C#–D across the two bars
  • Medium tempo (80–90) is more forgiving on intonation than very slow
🔔 Hand-pan · D minor Color
  • Use sparingly — a few struck notes per bar, not continuous
  • The sustain fills space naturally; trust it and leave gaps
  • Bar 3 (Dm7 resolution) is a natural moment to land a resonant note
  • The hand-pan's D minor scale already fits — follow your ear
🎼 Cello Texture · Drone
  • Open D and A strings are immediately available and fit the key
  • Slow bowing of D–A–D (double stop or arpeggiated) over Dm7 is enough
  • Imprecise intonation in this context reads as atmosphere, not error
  • Enter late (pass 6) so it feels like arrival rather than furniture
Recording & Production

Notes for Audacity

Track Order
  • Record octave mandolin chords first as guide track
  • Add regular mandolin melody second
  • Percussion third — lock to the chord track
  • Bass fourth — follow the cajon's downbeat
  • Hand-pan and cello last — they're color, added to taste
Panning
  • Cajon: center
  • Shakers: slight left (~L20)
  • Bass: center or slight right (~R15)
  • Octave mando: left (~L40)
  • Regular mando: right (~R40)
  • Hand-pan: slight right (~R25)
  • Cello: slight left (~L30)
EQ Priorities
  • Bass + cello: cut 200–300hz to reduce mud between them
  • Mandolins: gentle cut around 400hz if they sound boxy
  • Hand-pan: probably needs nothing — it records cleanly
  • Cajon: slight boost 80–100hz for body; cut 300hz
  • Do EQ before adding reverb, not after
Reverb
  • Use the same reverb type on everything — consistency makes it sound like one room
  • Less on bass and cajon (keeps low end tight)
  • More on hand-pan and cello (suits their sustain)
  • Medium room, not large hall — this is intimate music
USB / Hardware
  • A powered USB hub between Yeti and computer often fixes dodgy connections
  • Try a different USB cable first — they fail more often than the mic
  • In Audacity: set buffer size higher (512 or 1024) if you get clicks
  • Record one track at a time to reduce USB bandwidth
Mic Placement
  • Yeti cardioid mode for most instruments
  • Point at the soundhole from ~30–40cm for mandolin
  • For bass: aim at the upper bout, not the f-hole (less boomy)
  • Cajon: mic from behind (the port) for more body
  • Hand-pan: some distance (60cm+) — it's loud and rich up close
Open Questions

Still to Decide

? Does the song need a contrasting section (a B section in a different tonality or mood), or does the loop sustain interest on its own through arrangement and improvisation?
? Tempo: 70 bpm feels spacious and clear; 80–90 bpm would help bass intonation and make the bar-8 chromatic runs more natural. Worth recording both and comparing.
? Does the piece have a name? The D minor + hand-pan combination has a specific character — modal, slightly Middle-Eastern, chamber-like. A title would help fix the mood when recording.
? The cello is mostly untapped. Even one sustained drone note per pass would add significantly to the low-mid register. Worth experimenting before deciding it's not ready to use.
